I can completely agree that dropping a near-complete solution just a few days before the deadline would be disrespectful to the integrity of the leaderboard. But with a full month still left in the competition, this provides a fantastic foundation for the community to study, adapt, and build upon.

I feel your frustration. To be honest, when I suddenly realised I was dropping at a rate of 10 places every 5 minutes, I was in the middle of enjoying my lunch. It was really tasty, but I didn't finish it because I had to rush back to check what was going on. I think most of us who have spent countless nights tuning parameters, tweaking prompts, and designing multi-agent workflows can relate to that sinking feeling when a public notebook suddenly reshuffles the leaderboard.

That said, after calming down, I actually agree with this point: the race is relative, not absolute. If everyone gets the same lift from a public notebook, we're just fighting over smaller margins anyway.

But here is where it gets interesting. We are all assuming the notebook publisher is acting in good faith, sharing out of generosity. What if that is not always the case? What if there is a more cynical angle?

Here is a dark thought that has lingered in my mind for a long time.

Imagine it is the final hour of the competition. The top 5 are locked in for prize money, and you are sitting at 6th place, just a few points away but with no realistic chance to break into the top 5 anymore. You have a killer private solution that you have been saving all along. You know that if you publish it right now, in the last 30 minutes, there is no way everyone can fully integrate it in time. But a few teams right below you might copy and merge it and leapfrog the top 5. You will not win anything anyway, so why not?

You publish the notebook. Chaos ensues. The top 5 scramble to merge it, but the clock runs out. The leaderboard freezes with a completely different set of names at the top. You still get nothing, but you just turned the entire prize distribution into a lottery. All because you had nothing to lose.

Has this ever happened? I genuinely do not know. But the fact that the system makes it technically possible, and the incentives could align under the right conditions, makes me wonder whether Kaggle should at least consider a "no public submissions in the final 6 hours" rule—not for fairness, but just to prevent that one moment of spite from ruining everything for everyone else.

Or maybe I am just overthinking, and people are inherently good. What do you all think? 😅
