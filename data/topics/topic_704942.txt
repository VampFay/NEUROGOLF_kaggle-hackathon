ChatGPT Plus — $20/month — is all you need for this competition.

To my fellow Kagglers:

ChatGPT Plus — $20/month — is all you need for this competition.

My bio says I love ChatGPT and that I’m a devoted OpenAI fan. That is not joke. It is literally how I work.

In almost every chat, I either get help or learn something new from ChatGPT.

When people ask me on LinkedIn how to improve their Kaggle performance, my answer is always simple:

Keep asking ChatGPT.

For NeuroGolf, I used only ChatGPT Plus until I reached 7600. Only this week I finally upgrade to ChatGPT Pro, hoping to chase an even better result.

As Yiheng said, you can simply use ChatGPT in the web interface: copy-paste optimizations, run multiple tabs in parallel, and iterate constantly. That alone may be enough to get gold in this competition.

It is not fancy. But it works.

You only need trust ChatGPT. OpenAI is so nice and generous, as long as you use ChatGPT through the web app rather than the API, you can use it extensively without worrying about per-call costs.

This thread matches my experience — the LLM is a fantastic candidate generator, but the thing that ended up mattering most for me was the gap between "matches the visible grids" and "survives the held-out." I went the compile-the-rule direction (turning each task's transformation into a small ONNX graph rather than letting a net fit the examples), and what surprised me is how often a graph that's exact on every shown example still collapses on the hidden set — it captured the demonstration, not the rule. So I've started treating the visible examples as necessary-but-not-sufficient and running an equivariance check before trusting a candidate. For those of you reaching 7000+ with the LLM loop, how do you guard against a net that nails the visible examples but overfits the held-out — do you lean on the LB as the oracle, or screen for generalization before spending a submission?

Hi @shtljw , nice to see you here.

For me it is usually both. I try to understand the task first, then use ChatGPT as a thinking partner to explore ideas, write prompts and implementations, and keep iterating.

I don’t think it works best as “just give GPT the task and wait for the answer.” It can easily get lazy or wander around and waste tokens or time. The human side still matters a lot: choosing directions, judging whether a method makes sense, and deciding what to try next.

So my general workflow is: understand → ask GPT for ideas → learn from the result → refine again. The real power is in the iteration.
